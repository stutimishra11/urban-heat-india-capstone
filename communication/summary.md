# Summary of Findings

## 50-Word Summary Statement

Analysis of 30 years of NASA POWER temperature data (1995–2025), Census of India population figures, Global Forest Watch tree cover data, and Yale India Climate Opinion Maps challenge the assumption that Delhi is India's most alarming urban heat story. Using temperature anomalies — each city measured against its own 30-year baseline — Bengaluru's rate of relative warming emerges as a significant and underreported risk, driven by rapid urbanisation, collapse of green cover, and a perception gap obscured by media coverage focused on Delhi's dramatic but already-expected absolute heat peaks.

## Plain-Language Summary

When you read about heat in India, it is almost always about Delhi. This project asks a different question: compared to what each city used to be, which one has changed more?

Using four datasets — NASA satellite temperature data, Census of India population figures, Global Forest Watch tree cover loss, and Yale climate opinion surveys — the project finds that Bengaluru shows a warming trend that its mild-weather reputation obscures.
